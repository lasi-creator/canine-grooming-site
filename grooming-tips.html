Grooming Tips

Good grooming is a lifestyle, not a need.

Cleaning Thoughts


  • Grooming is a skill that can be learned, not a natural talent. It starts with a new way of thinking about what your hound is dealing with, and what they should have. Think of yourself as a barber serving a costomer. Consider the dogs individual needs.

  • Do not think of your grooming plan as a one-fits-all model, but rather, a ever-adapting strategy. If you stick to one grooming style, you will encounter cases you won't be prepared for. The key to success is a routine recollection on your daily situations.

  • Don't be afraid to fail. Don't be afraid to try again. Don't let impulsive urges lead you to become frustrated at your results, and instead, allow yourself to try and see it as a learning experience.

Class Materials

Use the following as a material guideline for our class:

  • Brushes, such as bristle brushes, slicker brushes, and pin brushes
  • Shampoos, such as puppy shampoo and tick & flea shampoos
  • Driers, like towels and hairdryers.
  • Blades, such as skip-tooth blades and curved comb blades.

Make sure any materials you bring or use is medically safe for the dogs you are grooming.

Try to be as gentle, yet firm, as possible when dealing with knots, tangles, and other complications you might encounter.

Weekly Advice

Use the following advice this week as a beginning platform for basic grooming habits:

  • Regular brushing: This is crucial to remove loose hair, distribute natural oils, and prevent mats, especially for dogs with long or thick coats; frequency depends on your dog's breed and coat type.
  • Nail trimming: Trim your dog's nails regularly to maintain their comfort and mobility; be careful not to cut too short and hit the quick.
  • Positive reinforcement: Make grooming a positive experience by using treats, praise, and gentle handling to help your dog associate grooming with something pleasant.
